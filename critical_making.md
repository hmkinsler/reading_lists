# Critical Making

## Rationale
This list takes up scholarship in critical making, which Ratto describes as “a mode of materially productive engagement that is intended to bridge the gap between creative physical and conceptual exploration” (2011, p. 252). In particular, this list explores how critical making acts as a methodological practice employed by scholars to interrogate technology through embodied prototyping and creation of material texts. This list thus seeks to explore what it means to identify oneself as a maker-scholar, considering how scholars document and write about their maker projects to make them visible as acts of scholarly production. Furthermore, it maps out the various outcomes of the production and circulation of these maker projects. To do so, the first section of the list explores foundational scholarship on critical making, considering how it has been defined and theorized by scholars working across the humanities (i.e. Garnet Hertz, Matt Ratto). The next section explores what it means to be a maker-scholar, considering the implications of identifying oneself as a maker and aligning with the DIY ethos that has driven maker culture both within and outside of academic spaces, while also accounting for the embodied experience of tinkering, working with materials, and developing creative projects as a scholarly practice. In particular, as an autistic scholar, this section allows me to consider how my own situatedness (Hamraie & Fritch, 2019; Haraway, 1991) plays a role in my own critical engagement with the technologies and materials that both shape my pedagogy (Cipolla, 2019; Richterich, 2022) and scholarship. The following sections then explore the rhetorical nature of critical making by surveying a variety of maker projects that hack (Beltrán, 2023; Huang, 2017; Murillo, 2025; Wark, 2004), create hardware and software to create new technologies or trouble those that already exist, or otherwise draw on critical making practices as a form of communicative expression (Faris & Holmes, 2022; Rieder, 2017).

Ultimately, this list seeks to ground myself in scholarship that captures the multifaceted nature of critical making as a scholarly practice and process, particularly as that process is embodied by makers themselves, informed and shaped by makers’ epistemological and embodied perspectives, and culminates in a maker text that carries its own implications for how it is received by a multitude of audiences.
